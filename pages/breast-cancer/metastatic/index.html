Understanding Metastatic Breast Cancer

Metastatic breast cancer (also called stage IV or advanced breast cancer) is breast cancer that has spread beyond the breast to other organs in the body. Learn about diagnosis, treatment, and living with metastatic breast cancer.

What is Metastatic Breast Cancer?

Metastatic breast cancer occurs when cancer cells spread beyond the breast to other parts of the body, most commonly to the:

  • Bones
  • Liver
  • Lungs
  • Brain

Key Facts About Metastatic Breast Cancer

  • It can occur at any age and can affect both women and men
  • About 30% of people diagnosed with early-stage breast cancer will develop metastatic disease
  • Some people have metastatic breast cancer when first diagnosed (called de novo metastatic breast cancer)
  • While currently not curable, it is treatable, and many people live for years with good quality of life

Signs and Symptoms

Bone Metastasis

  • Bone pain
  • Fractures
  • High calcium levels

Liver Metastasis

  • Yellowing of skin
  • Abdominal pain
  • Loss of appetite

Lung Metastasis

  • Shortness of breath
  • Chronic cough
  • Chest pain

Brain Metastasis

  • Headaches
  • Vision changes
  • Memory problems

Diagnosis and Monitoring

Common Diagnostic Tests

  • Blood tests
  • Imaging tests (CT scans, PET scans, bone scans)
  • Biopsies of suspected metastases
  • Tumor marker tests

Monitoring Treatment Response

Your healthcare team will regularly monitor your response to treatment through:

  • Physical exams
  • Blood tests
  • Imaging scans
  • Symptom assessment

Treatment Options

Systemic Treatments

  • Hormone therapy
  • Chemotherapy
  • Targeted therapy
  • Immunotherapy

Local Treatments

  • Surgery
  • Radiation therapy
  • Ablation

Treatment Goals

  • Extend life
  • Maintain quality of life
  • Manage symptoms
  • Slow cancer growth
