Diagnosing Breast Cancer

Understanding your breast cancer diagnosis is an important step in making treatment decisions. Learn about diagnostic tests, biopsies, pathology reports, and factors that affect treatment options.

Diagnostic Tests

Imaging Tests

Diagnostic Mammogram

A more detailed mammogram that focuses on a specific area of concern.

Breast Ultrasound

Uses sound waves to create images of breast tissue and can help distinguish between solid masses and fluid-filled cysts.

Breast MRI

Creates detailed images using magnetic fields and can be especially helpful for women at high risk.

Breast Biopsy

A biopsy is the only definitive way to diagnose breast cancer. During a biopsy, tissue or fluid is removed from the breast and examined under a microscope.

Types of Biopsies

Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA)

Uses a thin needle to remove fluid or cells from a breast lump.

Core Needle Biopsy

Uses a larger needle to remove small cylinders of tissue from the abnormal area.

Surgical Biopsy

Removes part or all of the suspicious area through surgery.

Understanding Pathology Reports

Cancer Type

Ductal Carcinoma In Situ (DCIS)

Non-invasive breast cancer confined to the ducts.

Invasive Ductal Carcinoma (IDC)

Cancer that has spread beyond the ducts into surrounding tissue.

Invasive Lobular Carcinoma (ILC)

Cancer that begins in the lobules and can spread to other parts of the breast.

Tumor Grade

Tumor grade describes how abnormal the cancer cells look compared to normal cells.

Grade 1

Well-differentiated cells that look most like normal cells.

Grade 2

Moderately differentiated cells.

Grade 3

Poorly differentiated cells that look very different from normal cells.

Hormone Receptor Status

Estrogen Receptor (ER)

Indicates if the cancer cells grow in response to estrogen.

Progesterone Receptor (PR)

Shows if the cancer cells grow in response to progesterone.

HER2 Status

Determines if the cancer cells have too much HER2 protein.

Cancer Staging

Staging describes the extent of cancer in your body. It helps determine your prognosis and treatment options.

Stage 0

Non-invasive breast cancer (DCIS)

Stage I

Early-stage invasive breast cancer

Stage II

Cancer is larger or has spread to lymph nodes

Stage III

Locally advanced breast cancer

Stage IV

Metastatic breast cancer that has spread to other parts of the body

Factors Affecting Treatment

  • Stage of the cancer
  • Tumor grade
  • Hormone receptor status
  • HER2 status
  • Age and menopausal status
  • Overall health
  • Personal preferences

Getting a Second Opinion

Getting a second opinion is common and can help you feel more confident about your diagnosis and treatment plan.

Tips for Getting a Second Opinion

  • Don't worry about offending your doctor
  • Ask for copies of all your medical records and test results
  • Check with your insurance about coverage
  • Consider seeking opinions at major cancer centers

Support During Diagnosis

A breast cancer diagnosis can be overwhelming. Support is available to help you through this time.

Emotional Support

  • Support groups in Bellevue
  • One-on-one counseling
  • Online communities

Practical Support

  • Patient navigators
  • Transportation assistance
  • Financial counseling
